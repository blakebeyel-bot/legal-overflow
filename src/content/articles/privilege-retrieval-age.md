Work-product doctrine protects the attorney's thought process. Attorney-client privilege protects the attorney's conversations with the client. Both doctrines assume an attorney whose thoughts live in a human skull and whose conversations happen in rooms with doors.

Neither assumption is still load-bearing.

When an attorney drafts a brief by iterating thirty-four prompts against a frontier model — revising, refining, occasionally arguing with the tool — those prompts constitute *something*. The question is what.
