The call came at 4:37 on a Wednesday. Opposing counsel, a partner at a firm I had negotiated against maybe a dozen times, wanted to talk through §7. Not the economic terms — those were settled. The liability cap. The carve-outs. The limitation-on-damages language that, in my experience, is where the real fight always lives.

"I ran it through Claude," he said. "Same thing you did."

I knew, because I had been using the same tool for three months on every transactional matter that crossed my desk. I had assumed I was alone in that corner of the practice.

## The convergence problem

When two sides of a negotiation are consulting identical tools — tools trained on the same corpus, prompted in substantially similar ways by two attorneys who both read the same law-review articles on AI-assisted practice — the redlines converge. Not perfectly, but closer than any two human attorneys would produce independently.

In the four transactions I've tracked since, the pattern holds: the model-assisted markups agree on roughly 73% of substantive changes. The 27% that remains becomes the entire negotiation.

## What gets flattened

The space that disappears is not adversarial in nature. It is the space of *craft* — the clause a senior partner drafted in 2014 because a deal almost blew up over it, the phrasing a client insists on for reasons nobody remembers, the indemnity architecture that exists because someone's uncle died in a helicopter crash on a drilling platform in 1997.

These textures do not survive a frontier model's editorial pass. The model, given no context, will always prefer the mean.

## The questions I don't have answers to

1. If both sides use the same tool, is that a shared authority — or a shared bias?
2. When the model suggests language neither side would have drafted unprompted, whose interests does that language serve?
3. What does "zealous representation" mean when the zeal gets smoothed out in the first pass?

I have been writing this article for six weeks. I do not know how it ends.
