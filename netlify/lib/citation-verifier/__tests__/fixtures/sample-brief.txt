The Supreme Court has long held that the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees
equal protection. See Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483, 495
(1954). The Court reaffirmed this principle in subsequent decisions.
Id. at 495. See also Loving v. Virginia, 388 U.S. 1 (1967).

Plaintiffs rely on 42 U.S.C. § 1983 to bring this action against state
actors. The implementing regulations at 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g) define the
relevant terms. See also 29 U.S.C. §§ 201-219 (the FLSA).

The Constitution itself addresses this directly. U.S. Const. art. I, § 8,
cl. 3. The Fourteenth Amendment, of course, applies to the states.
U.S. Const. amend. XIV.

Lower courts have followed suit. See, e.g., Smith v. Jones, 123 F.3d 456,
460 (2d Cir. 2019); Doe v. Roe, 100 F. Supp. 3d 200 (S.D.N.Y. 2015).
California courts have reached similar conclusions. People v. Garcia,
50 Cal. 4th 100 (2010); see also People v. Ramirez, 200 Cal. App. 4th
500, 510 (2011). New York is no different. People v. Smith, 25 N.Y.3d 1
(2015).
